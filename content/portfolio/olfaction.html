A tunnel into the brain.

“FROM many directions, workers are tunnelling hopefully into the mountain, some with steam shovels and others with dental drills. Some travel blindly in a circle and come out close to their point of entrance; some connect, usually in a mismatched fashion, with the burrows of others. Some have chosen to disregard the random activities of their fellows and have worked out in a small region an elegant system of tunnels of their own. Both the attraction and confusion of this multitudinous excavation lie in the fact that none of these workers knows precisely what they are looking for, or what they will find.” (Kenneth Roeder)

The fly’s sense of smell is a compact, well-mapped circuit, which makes it a good place to ask how a wiring diagram gives rise to behaviour. Much of my own work has focused on the lateral horn, a brain region thought to drive innate responses to odour.

The Drosophila olfactory system

Odours are detected by olfactory receptor neurons on the antennae and maxillary palps. Each receptor type converges on its own glomerulus in the antennal lobe, where projection neurons collect the signal and carry it to two higher centres: the mushroom body, which supports learned associations, and the lateral horn, thought to drive innate responses.

Odour encoding and decoding

An odour is represented by the combination of glomeruli it activates, a combinatorial code that projection neurons transform before it reaches the higher centres. Reading that transformation is one way to connect circuit structure to what the fly smells.

Odour scenes

In the wild, odours arrive as turbulent, intermittent plumes and mixtures rather than clean single stimuli. Making sense of these natural odour scenes is a central challenge for the system.
